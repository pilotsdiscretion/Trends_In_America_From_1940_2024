Diet & Nutrition

The American diet has shifted dramatically since 1940 — from home-cooked whole foods toward commercially processed, calorie-dense products. The introduction of high-fructose corn syrup (HFCS) in the 1970s, combined with agribusiness subsidies, dramatically altered caloric intake and food composition.

Key Products & Controversies
Glyphosate (Roundup): World's most-used herbicide. IARC classified "probably carcinogenic" (Group 2A) 2015. EPA maintains "not likely carcinogenic." EU re-approved through 2033 over EFSA/WHO split. California Prop 65 warning required.
Neonicotinoids (imidacloprid, clothianidin, thiamethoxam): Systemic insecticides linked to bee colony collapse. Banned EU 2018 for outdoor use. Syngenta, Bayer primary producers.
Atrazine: #2 herbicide in U.S. (corn belt). Banned EU since 2004. Linked to endocrine disruption in amphibians (Tyrone Hayes/UC Berkeley research; Syngenta attempted to discredit research — documented in court records).
Paraquat: Rapidly lethal herbicide. Banned in EU, UK, China, Brazil, 50+ countries. Still registered in U.S. (EPA). Linked to Parkinson's disease. Syngenta principal manufacturer.

Adult obesity has tripled since 1960, accelerating sharply after 1980. The shift correlates with changes in diet, food policy, physical activity, sleep disruption, and exposure to endocrine-disrupting chemicals. No single cause is universally agreed upon.

Obesity by Demographics & Geography

Adult obesity (BMI ≥ 30) reached 40.3% in 2021–23. It is not evenly distributed: women are slightly more affected than men, it peaks in middle age, Black and Hispanic adults have the highest rates (Asian adults the lowest), and it is concentrated in the South and Appalachia — West Virginia leads the nation. Average adult BMI has climbed from ~25 (a "normal" reading) in the early 1960s to nearly 30 today.

Overall Mortality

Age-adjusted mortality declined dramatically across the 20th century — from ~1,785 deaths per 100,000 in 1940 to ~722 in 2024 — driven by antibiotic use, improved sanitation, reduced smoking, better cardiac care, and public health infrastructure. The COVID-19 pandemic caused the largest single-year reversal in decades.

⚠️ Direct-to-Consumer Pharmaceutical Advertising: A Near-Global Ban

Direct-to-consumer (DTC) pharmaceutical advertising — TV commercials, print ads, and digital campaigns that market prescription drugs directly to patients rather than to doctors — is banned in virtually every country in the world. Only two nations permit it:

🇺🇸 United States — print DTC since 1981; broadcast DTC since 1997 (FDA rule change)
🇳🇿 New Zealand — DTC permitted since 1981 under Medicines Act

Countries that explicitly prohibit DTC pharma advertising include: Canada, the United Kingdom, the European Union (all 27 member states), Australia, Japan, South Korea, China, India, and the rest of the OECD. The World Health Organization, the EU, and medical associations including the AMA have called for DTC bans or stricter limits, citing evidence that DTC advertising drives unnecessary prescriptions, inflates drug costs, and shifts R&D toward lifestyle and chronic-condition drugs over treatments for rare diseases and infections.

Psychiatric Institutionalization & Deinstitutionalization

State psychiatric hospitals housed over 558,000 patients at their 1955 peak — a rate of 339 per 100,000 Americans. Deinstitutionalization, driven by the introduction of chlorpromazine (1954), the 1963 Community Mental Health Act, Medicaid funding incentives, and civil liberties litigation, rapidly reduced that to ~37,000 by 2016 — but community mental health infrastructure was never adequately funded to replace it. Many former patients cycled into homelessness, jails, and prisons. Early institutionalization data shows women were committed at slightly higher rates than men — reflecting both genuine prevalence differences and, historically, non-consensual commitment for social non-conformity (e.g., "hysteria," "involutional melancholia"). This ratio reversed over time.

Fentanyl — The Third Wave of the Overdose Crisis

Illicitly manufactured fentanyl — a synthetic opioid up to 50× stronger than heroin — turned a serious overdose problem into a mass-casualty event. Casual Dining: Boom, Buyouts & Bust

The sit-down chain — the "bar and grill," the themed steakhouse, the endless breadsticks — boomed from the 1990s into the 2000s, then entered a long slide often called the "casual dining recession." Squeezed between cheaper fast-casual upstarts, third-party delivery, and rising costs, traffic at full-service chains has fallen for a decade. Private equity accelerated the reckoning: firms bought chains, loaded them with debt and sale-leaseback deals, and several later collapsed into bankruptcy (Red Lobster and TGI Fridays both filed Chapter 11 in 2024). Meanwhile the food itself shifted — like fast food — toward centrally prepped, frozen and par-cooked components reheated on site, even as menus advertise "made fresh."

Male Neonatal Circumcision

Circumcision of male newborns was rare in the US before World War II but spread rapidly as hospital births became standard and medical organizations endorsed it. Rates peaked around 1981 at roughly 65% of male newborns and have gradually declined since, with significant regional variation (the West runs 20–25 points below the Midwest/South). The American Academy of Pediatrics reversed its recommendation in 1971, re-endorsed it cautiously in 2012, and research continues on net health effects. NHANES surveys of adult males aged 14–59 (2005–10) found ~80.5% overall circumcision prevalence — higher than neonatal rates due to procedures performed in childhood, adolescence, or adulthood, and older cohorts.

Same-Sex Marriage Legalization

Massachusetts became the first US state to legalize same-sex marriage in May 2004, following the Goodridge v. Department of Public Health ruling. Legalization spread through court rulings and, later, state legislatures and referenda — the first time popular votes approved same-sex marriage anywhere in the world was in Maine, Maryland, and Washington in 2012. The Supreme Court's Obergefell v. Hodges decision on June 26, 2015 held that the Fourteenth Amendment requires all states to license and recognize same-sex marriages. The Respect for Marriage Act (December 2022) provided additional federal statutory protection.

Poverty in America

Official US poverty measurement began in 1959, when about 22% of Americans lived below the poverty line. The War on Poverty drove dramatic declines through the early 1970s; since then the official rate has cycled with the economy without returning to its 1973 low. The picture changes a lot depending on who you look at — poverty is far higher for children than for seniors, for Black, Hispanic and Native Americans than for White and Asian Americans, for women than for men, and in the Deep South and Southwest than in the Northeast and Upper Midwest. The Supplemental Poverty Measure (SPM), which counts taxes and benefits, shows how directly policy moves the numbers: it hit a record low of 7.8% in 2021 under expanded pandemic aid, then jumped to 12.9% in 2023 after that aid expired.

⚠️ Special Immigrant Visa Backlogs & Operation Allies Welcome
Both the Afghan and Iraqi SIV programs have faced severe administrative backlogs. As of the August 2021 Afghanistan withdrawal, an estimated 70,000–80,000 Afghans were in the SIV pipeline but had not yet received their visa — many were left behind. The U.S. subsequently airlifted ~76,000 Afghans under emergency humanitarian parole (Operation Allies Welcome), a temporary status that did not provide a direct path to LPR. The Afghan Adjustment Act (introduced 2022, not yet passed as of 2025) would allow these parolees to apply for LPR status. Similarly, the Iraqi SIV program, which ran from 2009–2021, faced processing times of 3–5 years for many applicants. Congress has reauthorized and expanded both programs multiple times, but demand has consistently exceeded processing capacity. Veterans' advocacy groups including No One Left Behind and American Refugee Committee have documented thousands of SIV-eligible allies killed or harmed while awaiting visa processing.

American incomes have risen substantially in nominal terms since 1940, but inflation has eroded much of that gain — a dollar in 1940 had the purchasing power of roughly $22 in 2023. Homelessness in America

On a single night in January 2024, HUD counted 771,480 people experiencing homelessness — the highest number since national point-in-time (PIT) counts began in 2007, and an 18% jump over 2023, the largest single-year increase on record. The rise is driven by housing costs, the end of pandemic-era protections, and migration, layered on long-running structural factors. Homelessness is not evenly distributed: Black and Native Americans are heavily overrepresented, most people counted are men, and serious mental illness and substance-use disorders are far more common among homeless people — especially the chronically and unsheltered homeless — than in the general population, a legacy partly traceable to the deinstitutionalization charted in the Mental Health tab.

🏷️ Tariffs & Trade Duties

For most of the 1800s, tariffs (customs duties on imports) were the main source of federal revenue — 50% to 90% of the budget. The 1913 income tax ended that, and for a century tariffs faded to under 2% of revenue and ~1–2% average rates. That reversed abruptly in 2025: sweeping new tariffs pushed the average rate to ~14.5% — the highest since the 1930s — and customs revenue to a record ~$195 billion. In February 2026 the Supreme Court struck down the broadest ("reciprocal," IEEPA-based) tariffs, while industry-specific Section 232 tariffs (steel, aluminum, autos) remained and a 10% across-the-board duty was re-imposed under a different statute. Structural Shifts That Touch Every Tab

Deinstitutionalization → Homelessness Pipeline

In 1955, psychiatric hospitals held ~560,000 inpatients. The 1963 Community Mental Health Act launched mass discharge without adequate outpatient infrastructure. By 2020, fewer than 35,000 inpatient psychiatric beds remained. The discharged population did not vanish — they became the homeless and the incarcerated. Today roughly 30% of the 600,000 homeless population has serious mental illness, and jails have effectively replaced asylums as the largest mental health institutions in the country.

The Great Decoupling: Productivity vs. Wages

From 1940 to 1973, productivity and median wages rose in near-lockstep. Then they split: productivity continued climbing while median wages stagnated in real terms. Causes include Nixon ending the gold standard (1971), deregulation, globalization (NAFTA 1994), and declining union density (from ~35% to ~10%). The result: corporate profits and top incomes soared while the cost of housing, healthcare, and education far outpaced wage growth for the bottom 80%.

The Nutrition Transition

The American diet shifted from minimally processed whole foods (1940s–50s) to heavily processed foods (1960s–70s) to ultra-processed foods dominating over 57% of caloric intake by 2010. USDA's 1977 dietary guidelines — recommending low-fat, high-carbohydrate eating — arrived just as food manufacturers were refining food engineering for maximal palatability. The compounding effect: rising obesity (13% → 36%), type 2 diabetes, and metabolic disease, all largely preventable.

Secularization & Social Atomization

Church membership fell from ~76% (1950) to ~45% (2023). Religiously unaffiliated Americans rose from near zero (1944) to 29% (2023). Churches historically provided not just faith but community infrastructure: mutual aid networks, marriage markets, social norms, and crisis support. Their decline correlates with measurable rises in loneliness, declining civic participation, falling marriage rates, and rising deaths of despair. Gen Z is the most secular American generation ever recorded.

The LGBTQ+ Revolution

Sodomy was criminalized across the U.S. in 1940; homosexuality was classified as a mental disorder until 1973. From Stonewall (1969) to Lawrence v. Texas (2003) to Obergefell (2015), legal status transformed within one lifetime. Social identification followed: Gallup shows 7.6% of adults now identify as LGBTQ+ (2023), with Gen Z at ~22% — a shift so rapid it has no historical parallel in American public opinion polling.

Pharmaceutical Dependency

Americans went from minimal pharmaceutical use to the highest per-capita drug consumption in the world. Each wave reshaped behavior: tranquilizers in the 1950s, birth control in the 1960s, antidepressants/SSRIs in the 1990s, opioids in the 2000s (killing 80,000+/year at peak), and GLP-1 agonists today. The FDA approved OxyContin in 1996; by 2012 enough opioids were prescribed for every American adult to have their own bottle. The opioid epidemic represents one of the largest man-made public health catastrophes in U.S. history.

The Digital Revolution & Big Tech

Computing ran from IBM's mainframes (1950s–80s) to the PC (Microsoft, Intel, Apple), the internet (Amazon, Google), mobile & social (Apple, Meta), and now AI (Nvidia). A handful of firms — the "Magnificent Seven" — are together worth ~$22 trillion, with Nvidia the first company ever valued at $5 trillion (2025). Each era's leaders often vanished in the next (Wang, DEC, Nortel, Nokia, BlackBerry, Yahoo). Tech is now the most valuable industry on earth and the engine of the stock market.

Political Realignment

The "Solid South" went from the most Democratic region in the country to the most Republican after the 1960s civil-rights era, and the parties sorted ideologically. Self-identified independents rose to a record ~43% of adults. The Electoral College and popular vote diverged in 2000 and 2016. Turnout hit a modern high in 2020 (~66.6%). The Politics tab's state-by-state heatmap and picker trace the whole realignment.

The Automobile Century → Electrification

Cars built modern America — the Interstate system, the suburbs, and ~284 million registered vehicles. Foreign brands went from a novelty (the VW Beetle) to the majority of sales (Toyota, Honda, then Hyundai-Kia and German luxury). Body styles shifted from sedans to SUVs/trucks, vehicles grew heavier, and prices outpaced inflation. Now the industry is electrifying — from the crushed GM EV1 (1996) to Tesla and ~8% of new sales going electric.

Corporate Consolidation & Private Equity

Ownership of everyday America concentrated into a few holding companies and private-equity funds. Fast food rolled up under Roark/Inspire and 3G; casual-dining chains were bought, loaded with debt, and several collapsed (Red Lobster and TGI Fridays both filed Chapter 11 in 2024; Marie Callender's and Fuddruckers nearly vanished). The same playbook reshaped retail, healthcare, and housing.

Mass Incarceration

From the early 1970s, the War on Drugs, mandatory minimums and "tough on crime" laws drove the U.S. to the highest incarceration rate on earth — peaking near 755 per 100,000 (prison + jail) around 2008. Racial disparities are severe (Black Americans ~5× the White rate), recidivism is high, and jails became de-facto mental-health institutions. Rates have slowly declined since, sharply so during COVID.

Globalization → Tariffs & Deglobalization

Tariffs funded the government before 1913, then fell for a century as the U.S. led free-trade expansion (GATT, NAFTA, the WTO). That reversed sharply in 2018 and again in 2025, when sweeping tariffs pushed the average rate to ~14.5% — the highest since the 1930s — before the Supreme Court struck down the broadest measures in February 2026. The era of frictionless globalization is, for now, over.

Decade-by-Decade: The Biggest Inflection Points
Health Economics Social / Family Policy / Law Mental Health Culture
1940s
1944 Economics
GI Bill (Servicemen's Readjustment Act)
Subsidized college education and low-cost mortgages for ~8 million returning veterans. Drove the suburban homeownership explosion, pushed median education level up by years, and created the mid-century middle class. Homeownership rate jumped from 44% (1940) to 62% (1960).
1946–1964 Social
Baby Boom
78 million births in 18 years — the largest sustained birthrate surge in U.S. history (peaked at 3.77 children per woman in 1957). This cohort drove every major social institution they entered: schools, universities, the housing market, healthcare, and eventually Social Security and Medicare.
1940s Culture
Peak American Religiosity
~89% of Americans identified as Protestant or Catholic. Church attendance was a social norm rather than a personal choice. This era represents the high-water mark from which all subsequent religious affiliation data declines.
1950s
1955 Mental Health
Peak Institutionalization: 560,000 Psychiatric Inpatients
State psychiatric hospitals reached their maximum census. Conditions were frequently brutal — overcrowded warehouses with minimal treatment. The system's failure would catalyze deinstitutionalization, but the replacement community care system was never adequately funded.
1955 Culture
McDonald's Opens; Fast Food Era Begins
Ray Kroc's franchising model scaled nationally within a decade. Combined with the 1954 mass adoption of TV (bringing food advertising into homes), this began reorienting the American diet toward cheap, calorie-dense, convenient processed food — setting up the obesity epidemic 25 years later.
1950s Health
Smoking at Peak: ~45% of Adults
Cigarette consumption reached its historic high. The 1964 Surgeon General's Report on smoking and cancer had not yet arrived. Lung cancer, which was rare before widespread cigarette use, was about to become the leading cancer killer for men — and eventually women.
1960s
1960 Policy
FDA Approves the Oral Contraceptive Pill
Within 5 years, 6.5 million American women were on the pill. Fertility rates began their long decline toward and below replacement level. Women's workforce participation rose, marriage age rose, and family structure began its decades-long transformation.
1963 Mental Health
Community Mental Health Act — Deinstitutionalization Begins
President Kennedy's landmark legislation ordered the closure of large state psychiatric hospitals in favor of local community mental health centers. The hospitals closed; the community centers were chronically underfunded. Hospital census fell from 560k to <35k beds by 2020 — releasing hundreds of thousands with no support structure.
1965 Policy
Medicare, Medicaid & the Immigration Act
The Great Society's two most enduring programs: Medicare and Medicaid brought healthcare to the elderly and poor. The same year, the Immigration and Nationality Act ended national-origin quotas — opening immigration to Asia, Africa, and Latin America, fundamentally changing the country's demographic composition over the next 60 years.
1969 Social
Stonewall Riots — LGBTQ+ Rights Movement Ignites
Police raids on the Stonewall Inn in New York City sparked days of protests and galvanized the modern gay rights movement. Homosexuality was still classified as a mental disorder (until 1973) and criminalized under sodomy laws (until 2003). The distance from Stonewall to Obergefell (2015) represents one of the fastest normative transformations in American legal and social history.
1970s
1971 Economics
Nixon Ends the Gold Standard
The U.S. abandonment of the Bretton Woods gold peg ushered in the modern fiat currency era. It gave governments unprecedented flexibility — and removed the anchor that had historically constrained inflation. The 1970s stagflation crisis (inflation hitting 14% by 1980) was the immediate consequence; the long-term consequence was decades of currency debasement that eroded savings and purchasing power.
1969–1979 Social
Divorce Rate Doubles — No-Fault Divorce Spreads
California's 1969 no-fault divorce law spread to all 50 states by 1985. Divorce rates roughly doubled from the mid-1960s to their 1981 peak. Single-parent households (especially single-mother households) rose sharply, with lasting effects on child poverty, educational outcomes, and the next generation's relationship with marriage.
1973 Economics
Arab Oil Embargo — Stagflation Era
OPEC's oil embargo quadrupled oil prices. Combined with loose monetary policy, the U.S. entered a decade of simultaneous high inflation and high unemployment — "stagflation" — that defied Keynesian economics. Real wages fell, and the era of post-war prosperity effectively ended. The 1979 Iranian Revolution doubled oil prices again.
1978–1982 Economics
Volcker Shock — Interest Rates Hit 20%
Fed Chair Paul Volcker raised the federal funds rate to 20% to crush inflation. It worked — but at the cost of the worst recession since the 1930s (unemployment hit 10.8%). Housing sales collapsed. The victory over inflation set the stage for the 1980s expansion, but the short-term pain was severe, particularly for farmers and manufacturing workers.
1980s
1981 Economics
Reagan Tax Cuts & Deregulation — Income Inequality Inflects
The Economic Recovery Tax Act of 1981 cut the top marginal rate from 70% to 50% (later 28%). Combined with financial deregulation and declining union density, this marks the clearest inflection point in income inequality data: the share of income flowing to the top 1% began a sustained rise from ~9% to ~20%+ by 2015. Median wages stagnated in real terms.
1981 Health
AIDS Epidemic Identified
The CDC published its first report on what would become known as AIDS in June 1981. By 2020, ~700,000 Americans had died of AIDS-related illness. The epidemic reshaped sexuality, transformed public health infrastructure, accelerated LGBTQ+ political organizing, and — through stigma and the Reagan administration's delayed response — became one of the most politically charged public health crises in American history.
1980s Health
Obesity Rate Begins Its Rapid Climb
American obesity (BMI ≥30) sat near 13–15% through the 1970s, then began rising sharply — reaching 23% by 1994, 31% by 2000, and 36%+ by 2010. The timing correlates with mass adoption of ultra-processed food, USDA low-fat dietary guidelines (1977–80), larger portion sizes, declining physical labor, and increased sedentary screen time.
1984–1990 Policy
War on Drugs & Mandatory Minimums — Mass Incarceration Accelerates
Federal mandatory minimum sentencing and the Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1986 imposed severe sentences for crack cocaine (vs. powder cocaine) offenses. The U.S. prison population, already rising, accelerated sharply. Incarceration went from ~150 per 100k (1970s) to a peak of 756 per 100k (2008) — the highest incarceration rate in the world. Black men were incarcerated at 5–7× the rate of white men.
1990s
1994 Economics
NAFTA — Globalization Accelerates; Manufacturing Declines
The North American Free Trade Agreement eliminated tariffs between the U.S., Mexico, and Canada. Manufacturing employment, already declining, accelerated its fall — particularly in the Midwest "Rust Belt." Real wages in manufacturing stagnated. Trade economists have since documented substantial localized employment and wage damage in import-competing regions, contributing to the economic anxiety that shaped 2000s and 2010s politics.
1996 Policy
Welfare Reform (PRWORA) — Safety Net Restructured
The Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Reconciliation Act replaced AFDC with TANF, introducing time limits and work requirements. The welfare rolls fell dramatically. Proponents credit rising employment; critics note that deep poverty (below $2/day) increased for the most vulnerable families while the system lost its ability to respond counter-cyclically in recessions.
1999 Economics
Gramm-Leach-Bliley Repeals Glass-Steagall
The Depression-era separation between commercial banking and investment banking was eliminated. Banks could now underwrite and sell mortgage-backed securities while also taking deposits. This deregulation was a direct precondition for the 2008 financial crisis: banks accumulated enormous mortgage risk they had inadequate capital to absorb when housing prices fell.
1990s Culture
"Nones" Emerge; Mainline Protestant Collapse Begins
The GSS first detected a statistically meaningful rise in religiously unaffiliated Americans in the early 1990s. Gen X was measurably less religious than Boomers. Mainline Protestant denominations — Methodist, Presbyterian, Episcopal, Lutheran — saw decades of continuous membership decline that has not reversed. Evangelical denominations initially held; their politicization would later accelerate disaffiliation among young people.
2000s
2001 Policy
September 11 — Wars, Debt, and Mass Trauma
The attacks killed ~3,000 people and triggered wars in Afghanistan and Iraq costing $3+ trillion and ~7,000 U.S. military deaths. Veterans returned with PTSD and TBI at unprecedented rates. The surveillance state expanded dramatically. Islamophobia rose sharply. The long-run fiscal cost contributed to the structural deficits that now constrain domestic spending.
2000s Health
Opioid Epidemic — Pharmaceutical Industry Trigger
Purdue Pharma's misleading marketing of OxyContin (approved 1996) drove opioid prescriptions to 255 million/year by 2012 — enough for every American adult to have their own bottle. Opioid overdose deaths rose from ~4,000 (1999) to 50,000+/year by 2016, then continued rising as supply shifted to illicit fentanyl. Total opioid deaths since 2000 exceed 500,000 — more than all U.S. combat deaths in WWII.
2007–2009 Economics
Financial Crisis — $11 Trillion in Household Wealth Destroyed
The collapse of the U.S. housing bubble triggered the worst financial crisis since the Great Depression. Home values fell 30% nationally; some markets fell 50%+. Homeownership rates fell from 69% (2004) to 63% (2016). The recovery was slow and uneven — the top quintile recovered fastest while many middle-class families permanently lost their primary store of wealth. Black and Hispanic families, who had made historic homeownership gains, were hit hardest.
2003 & 2004 Policy
Lawrence v. Texas; Massachusetts Legalizes Same-Sex Marriage
The Supreme Court struck down sodomy laws in all 14 remaining states (2003). A year later, Massachusetts became the first state to legalize same-sex marriage — 11 years before Obergefell made it nationwide. Public support for same-sex marriage jumped from 27% (1996) to 60%+ (2015) and 71% (2023) — one of the fastest public opinion shifts ever recorded on a major social issue.
2010s
2010 Health
Affordable Care Act — 20+ Million Newly Insured
The ACA was the largest expansion of health insurance coverage since Medicare/Medicaid (1965). Uninsurance rates fell from ~18% to ~9% by 2016. The law prohibited denial of coverage for pre-existing conditions, extended Medicaid to 138% of the poverty line in participating states, and allowed young adults to stay on parental insurance until 26. Despite ongoing political opposition, its core provisions remain in place.
2015 Policy
Obergefell v. Hodges — Nationwide Same-Sex Marriage
The Supreme Court ruled 5–4 that same-sex couples have a constitutional right to marry. 37 states had already legalized it by then, but the ruling made it uniform across all 50. LGBTQ+ identification has continued rising since — from ~3.5% (2012) to 7.6% (2023) — as social acceptance reduced stigma around disclosure.
2016–2017 Health
Life Expectancy Declines — "Deaths of Despair"
U.S. life expectancy fell for three consecutive years (2015–2017) — the first sustained decline since the 1918 flu pandemic. Princeton economists Anne Case and Angus Deaton documented "deaths of despair" among middle-aged white Americans without college degrees: drug overdoses, alcoholic liver disease, and suicide. The phenomenon reflects decades of economic dislocation, community collapse, and loss of social purpose.
2017 Culture
#MeToo — Cultural Reckoning on Sexual Harassment
Following Harvey Weinstein's exposure, the #MeToo movement triggered a rapid renegotiation of norms around sexual harassment and assault in the workplace. High-profile figures across media, politics, academia, and entertainment faced consequences for previously tolerated behavior. Gender dynamics in professional settings, STD testing practices, and social norms around sexuality all shifted measurably in subsequent years.
2020s
2020 Health
COVID-19 Pandemic — 1.1 Million Deaths & Mental Health Crisis
The worst pandemic in a century killed over 1.1 million Americans — disproportionately elderly, obese, and Black and Hispanic individuals. Lockdowns triggered a mental health emergency: anxiety, depression, and youth suicide attempts spiked. Remote work became permanent for millions, reshaping housing demand and urban economies. Life expectancy fell to 76.4 years (2021) — back to 1996 levels.
2021–2023 Economics
Inflation Surge — Worst Since 1981
CPI inflation peaked at 9.1% in June 2022 — driven by COVID-era supply chain disruptions, fiscal stimulus, and energy price shocks from Russia's Ukraine invasion. The Fed raised rates from near-zero to 5.25–5.5% in 18 months. Mortgage rates rose to 8%, compounding housing costs onto already-stretched incomes. Home affordability reached its worst level ever recorded (6.3× median income ratio).
2022 Policy
Dobbs v. Jackson — Roe v. Wade Overturned
The Supreme Court eliminated the federal constitutional right to abortion, returning regulation to states. Within months, 13 states enacted near-total abortion bans. The ruling reversed a 50-year federal framework. The social and demographic consequences — particularly for low-income women — are still unfolding, as are the political consequences visible in subsequent elections.
2023 Culture
Gen Z: Most Secular, Most LGBTQ+, Most Anxious Generation Recorded
By 2023: 29% of Americans are religiously unaffiliated (vs. near-zero in 1944); 7.6% identify as LGBTQ+ (22% of Gen Z). Gen Z also reports the highest rates of anxiety, depression, and loneliness ever recorded for a young American generation — a convergence of smartphone adoption (~2012), social media, economic insecurity, and the social isolation of COVID adolescence.
2022–2025 Culture
Generative-AI Boom & the Magnificent Seven
ChatGPT (late 2022) ignited a generative-AI race. AI-chip demand vaulted Nvidia to the first-ever $5 trillion valuation (2025); seven tech giants reached ~$22 trillion combined and drove most stock-market gains. AI began reshaping work, education, media, and software — the fastest technology-adoption curve yet recorded.
2025–2026 Economics
Tariff Shock & Deglobalization
Sweeping new tariffs pushed the average U.S. rate to ~14.5% — the highest since the 1930s — and customs revenue to a record ~$195B (FY2025). In February 2026 the Supreme Court struck down the broadest "reciprocal" tariffs, leaving sector tariffs (steel, aluminum, autos) in place. A decisive turn from the free-trade consensus of 1947–2016.
Additional Data Points Worth Tracking

Topics not yet in this dashboard that would meaningfully round out the picture:

Life Expectancy by Sex & Race The racial life expectancy gap (Black vs. white) has narrowed but persists at ~4–5 years. Men die ~5 years earlier than women. COVID widened both gaps significantly in 2020–21.
Suicide Rates by Sex Men die by suicide at 3–4× the rate of women despite women reporting more suicidal ideation. Male suicide is one of the largest unexplained sex-based health disparities. Rates have risen ~30% since 2000.
Smoking Rates One of public health's greatest victories: adult smoking fell from ~45% (1954) to ~12% (2023) after the 1964 Surgeon General's Report. Lung cancer in men peaked ~1990; in women ~2005, tracking 20-year lags.
Wealth & Income Inequality (Gini) The U.S. Gini coefficient rose from ~0.37 (1970) to ~0.49 (2022) — among the highest in the developed world. Top 1% wealth share doubled from ~22% to ~38%. Bottom 50% own ~2.5% of wealth.
Social Trust & Institutional Confidence Gallup confidence in major institutions (Congress, media, police, churches, banks) has fallen to historic lows across the board. Trust in newspapers: 51% (1979) → 18% (2023). Congress: 42% (1973) → 8% (2023).
Loneliness & Social Isolation Self-reported loneliness roughly doubled from the 1990s to 2020s. Surgeon General Vivek Murthy declared loneliness a public health epidemic in 2023. Average number of close friends has declined; time alone has risen, especially for men.
Physical Activity & Sedentary Behavior Only ~24% of adults meet federal physical activity guidelines. Sedentary leisure time (screen time) averages 10–12 hours/day. Labor-force physical activity fell as manufacturing jobs declined; service and knowledge work is largely sedentary.
Fertility & Total Fertility Rate TFR fell below the 2.1 replacement level in 1972 and has not recovered. By 2023 it sits near 1.62 — among the lowest ever recorded in the U.S. No peer nation that has fallen to sub-1.7 TFR has returned to replacement without immigration.
Labor Force Participation — Prime-Age Men The share of men aged 25–54 not working has risen from ~4% (1950s) to ~12% (2023). This "missing men" phenomenon is driven by disability, incarceration history, opioid use, and declining manufacturing jobs, with concentrated geographic impact.
Educational Attainment by Sex Women have outnumbered men in college enrollment since 1981 and now earn 57% of bachelor's degrees. The male–female educational gap has grown larger than the female–male gap was at its historic widest point, with under-studied downstream effects on earnings, marriage, and male mental health.
